How Daily Calorie Needs Are Calculated

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation calculates your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) — the calories your body burns at complete rest. The BMR is then multiplied by an activity factor to get your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE).

BMR (Male) = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age + 5 BMR (Female) = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age − 161 TDEE = BMR × Activity Factor Activity factors: Sedentary: × 1.2 Lightly active: × 1.375 Moderately active: × 1.55 Very active: × 1.725 Extra active: × 1.9
Example — Male, 30 years, 75 kg, 178 cm, moderately active

BMR = (10×75) + (6.25×178) − (5×30) + 5

BMR = 750 + 1112.5 − 150 + 5 = 1,717.5 kcal/day

TDEE = 1717.5 × 1.55 = 2,662 kcal/day

To lose ~0.5 kg/week: aim for ~2,162 kcal/day

Frequently Asked Questions

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation is considered the most accurate formula for estimating BMR and is the one recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. However, individual metabolism varies. Use the result as a starting point and adjust based on real-world weight changes over 2-4 weeks.
A caloric deficit of 500 kcal/day leads to approximately 0.5 kg of fat loss per week (since 1 kg of fat ≈ 7,700 kcal). Most health guidelines recommend not going below 1,200 kcal/day for women and 1,500 kcal/day for men. Rapid weight loss can cause muscle loss and nutritional deficiencies.
Macronutrients (macros) are protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Each gram of protein or carbs provides 4 kcal; each gram of fat provides 9 kcal. A common starting point: 30% protein, 40% carbs, 30% fat. Adjust based on goals — higher protein (35-40%) helps preserve muscle during weight loss.
