How Compound Interest Works

Compound interest means you earn interest on your interest — not just your initial deposit. Over time, this creates an exponential growth curve sometimes called the "8th wonder of the world" (attributed to Albert Einstein).

The key variables are: principal (P), annual rate (r), compounding frequency (n), and time in years (t).

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t) A = Final amount P = Principal (initial investment) r = Annual interest rate (as decimal, e.g. 0.07 for 7%) n = Compounding periods per year t = Time in years
Example — €1,000 at 7% for 30 years (monthly compounding)

A = 1000 × (1 + 0.07/12)^(12×30)

A = 1000 × (1.005833)^360

A ≈ €8,116 — your money grew 8× without adding anything extra!

Frequently Asked Questions

More frequent compounding means slightly more growth. Daily compounding earns marginally more than monthly, which earns more than annually. However, the difference between daily and monthly is small. The interest rate and time period matter far more than compounding frequency.
The Rule of 72 gives you a quick estimate of how long it takes to double your money: divide 72 by the annual interest rate. At 6%, you double every 12 years. At 9%, every 8 years. It's a mental math shortcut — this calculator gives the exact answer.
The S&P 500 has historically returned about 10% per year on average (7% adjusted for inflation). For conservative estimates, use 5-6%. For aggressive growth estimates, 9-10%. Always remember past performance doesn't guarantee future results.
