Frequently Asked Questions

BMI is a useful screening tool but has limitations. It doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat mass (athletes may have a high BMI despite low body fat), doesn't account for fat distribution, and may be less accurate for elderly people or different ethnic groups. It's a useful starting point, but consult a doctor for a full health assessment.
The WHO defines 18.5–24.9 as the healthy range. Most health guidelines recommend staying within this range. However, the optimal BMI for your specific situation may vary based on age, sex, muscle mass, and medical history. Speak with your GP for personalized guidance.
The WHO BMI categories are the same for both sexes. However, women naturally have a higher body fat percentage than men at the same BMI. Some researchers suggest gender-specific BMI ranges, but the standard WHO categories remain the most widely used reference.
