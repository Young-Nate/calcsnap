VAT Calculator Guide: How to Add or Remove VAT (With Examples)

Updated April 2026 • 10–12 min read

VAT (Value Added Tax) shows up everywhere: invoices, receipts, software subscriptions, imported goods, and services. If you work with pricing (or you’re just trying to understand a bill), you’ll often need to answer one of two questions:

  • “How do I add VAT to a net price?” (net → gross)
  • “How do I remove VAT from a gross price?” (gross → net)

This guide explains both, using simple formulas and practical examples you can reuse. If you prefer to skip the manual math, you can use CalcSnap’s VAT calculator to instantly add or remove VAT without mistakes.

What VAT is (and what “net” and “gross” mean)

Net price is the price before VAT is applied. Gross price is the price after VAT is added. The VAT rate is a percentage, such as 5%, 10%, 20%, or 23% depending on where you are and what you’re selling.

In many contexts:

  • Businesses quote net prices to other businesses (B2B) and then add VAT on the invoice.
  • Retail prices shown to consumers are often gross, meaning VAT is already included.

How to add VAT to a net price (net → gross)

To add VAT, you increase the net price by the VAT percentage.

The formula

gross = net × (1 + VAT rate)
VAT amount = net × VAT rate

Important: the VAT rate must be in decimal form. For example:

  • 20% VAT → 0.20
  • 5% VAT → 0.05
  • 23% VAT → 0.23

Example: add 20% VAT to 100

Net = 100, VAT rate = 0.20

  • VAT amount = 100 × 0.20 = 20
  • Gross = 100 × (1 + 0.20) = 100 × 1.20 = 120
Quick shortcut: Adding 20% VAT means multiplying by 1.20. Adding 5% VAT means multiplying by 1.05.

How to remove VAT from a gross price (gross → net)

Removing VAT is where many people make a mistake. You can’t simply subtract the VAT percentage from the gross price. You must divide by 1 + VAT rate0, because the gross price already includes VAT.

The formula

net = gross ÷ (1 + VAT rate)
VAT amount = gross − net

Example: remove 20% VAT from 120

Gross = 120, VAT rate = 0.20

  • Net = 120 ÷ 1.20 = 100
  • VAT amount = 120 − 100 = 20

Why subtracting 20% is wrong

If you subtract 20% directly from 120, you get 96. But 96 + 20% VAT is 115.20, not 120. The correct net is 100, because the “20%” is applied to net, not to gross.

Common VAT rates and multipliers

Multipliers make VAT calculations faster. Use these when adding VAT (net → gross):

  • 5% → multiply by 1.05
  • 10% → multiply by 1.10
  • 20% → multiply by 1.20
  • 23% → multiply by 1.23

And use these divisors when removing VAT (gross → net):

  • 5% → divide by 1.05
  • 10% → divide by 1.10
  • 20% → divide by 1.20
  • 23% → divide by 1.23

How to find the VAT rate (when you know net and gross)

Sometimes an invoice shows a net amount and a gross amount, and you want to confirm which VAT rate was applied.

The formula

VAT rate = (gross ÷ net) − 1

Example

Net = 250, Gross = 300

  • VAT rate = (300 ÷ 250) − 1 = 1.2 − 1 = 0.20 → 20%

How to calculate VAT amount only

You may only need the VAT amount (the tax portion). There are two reliable approaches:

1) If you have net and rate

VAT amount = net × VAT rate

2) If you have gross and rate

VAT amount = gross − (gross ÷ (1 + VAT rate))

This second method is especially useful when a receipt shows only the total paid (gross) and the rate.

Practical examples (pricing, discounts, and refunds)

Example 1: A net price with a discount, then VAT

Suppose your net price is 80 and you give a 10% discount before VAT. VAT is 20%.

  • Discounted net = 80 × (1 − 0.10) = 72
  • VAT amount = 72 × 0.20 = 14.40
  • Gross = 72 × 1.20 = 86.40

Example 2: A gross price and you need the net for accounting

A software subscription costs 49.20 including 20% VAT. Net is:

  • Net = 49.20 ÷ 1.20 = 41.00
  • VAT amount = 49.20 − 41.00 = 8.20

Example 3: Refunds (gross-to-gross vs net-to-net)

If you refund a customer the gross amount they paid, the VAT amount reverses automatically. If you’re issuing a credit note in net terms, make sure VAT is recalculated on the net credit at the same VAT rate.

Common VAT calculation mistakes to avoid

  • Subtracting VAT directly from gross. Always divide by 1 + rate0 to get net.
  • Mixing up percentage and decimal. 20% is 0.20, not 20.
  • Rounding too early. Round at the end unless local rules require line-by-line rounding.
  • Applying discounts in the wrong order. Confirm whether the discount is applied before VAT (typical) or after.

FAQ

Is VAT the same as sales tax?

They’re both consumption taxes, but they work differently. Sales tax is typically applied only at the final sale, while VAT is charged at each stage of the supply chain with credits for VAT paid on inputs.

How do I calculate 20% VAT quickly?

To add VAT: multiply net by 1.20. To remove VAT: divide gross by 1.20.

What if the VAT rate changes?

Use the same formulas with the updated percentage. The method never changes—only the rate does.
